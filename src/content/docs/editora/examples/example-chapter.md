# The Awakening of the Forest

The first light of morning filtered through the dense leaves of the ancient forest, painting the ground with golden patterns that danced in the gentle breeze. Aria opened her eyes slowly, as if the simple act of waking required a silent permission from the world around her.

She stood up, stretching against the gnarled trunk of an oak that had witnessed centuries of stories. Her long, calloused fingers — the mark of someone who had grown up climbing trees and blazing trails — found the rough texture of the bark, and for a moment, she could have sworn she felt an almost imperceptible vibration, as if the tree breathed in unison with her.

"Today is the day," she murmured, more to herself than to the forest.

But the forest, of course, was always listening.

## The Forgotten Trail

Aria followed the trail that no map recorded. Her bare feet knew every exposed root, every mossy stone, every spot where the lowest branches demanded a careful duck of the head. It was a dance she had performed since childhood, when her grandmother would take her by the hand and whisper secrets that the wind carried away.

*"The forest belongs to no one, but it welcomes us if we respect it."*

The words echoed in her mind as she moved forward. The air was thick with a scent she did not recognize — something between wildflowers and wet earth, but with a metallic note that made the hairs on the back of her neck stand on end.

And then she saw it.

In the clearing where there should have been only ferns and small shrubs, something glowed. Aria approached cautiously, her instincts screaming warnings that her curiosity refused to hear.

It was a sword.

Not an ordinary sword, like the ones the guards from the neighboring city carried with an air of disproportionate importance. This one was different. The blade, partially buried in the dark soil, emanated a bluish glow that pulsed at irregular intervals, like a weary heart. The hilt was wrapped in worn but still elegant leather, and at the center of the guard was a stone that seemed to hold a piece of the night sky.

Aria reached out her hand.

"Don't touch!"

The voice came from behind her, rough and urgent. She spun around in a leap, her body already in a defensive stance before her brain could process the stranger's presence.

It was a man — or at least, it appeared to be. His silver hair fell in disarray over broad shoulders, and his eyes... his eyes were the same color as the stone in the sword, a deep blue that seemed to see right through her.

"Who are you?" Aria asked, without lowering her guard.

The stranger smiled, but there was no humor in it.

"Someone who knows you shouldn't be here. And also knows that, unfortunately, you *needed* to be."

He pointed at the sword.

"That blade chose you, Aria of the Forest. And that changes everything."

## The Weight of a Choice

Aria took a step back, her eyes darting between the stranger and the sword still glowing on the ground.

"I don't know what you're talking about. And I didn't choose anything."

"The choice is never conscious," he replied, taking a step forward. "That's what makes it a *true* choice. If you could rationalize it, weigh pros and cons, it wouldn't be a choice. It would be a calculation."

He knelt beside the sword, his fingers hovering an inch from the blade without touching it.

"This is *Dawnlight*. Forged in the celestial smithies before the gods forgot how to create wonders. It has slept for a thousand years, waiting. And now it has awakened. Because of you."

Aria shook her head, trying to dispel the madness that seemed to have settled over the clearing.

"That's absurd. Swords don't choose people. And they definitely don't wait a thousand years for a girl who barely knows how to read maps, let alone fulfill prophecies or whatever this is."

The stranger laughed, a genuine sound this time.

"You have the gift of disbelief. Good. It'll keep you alive when things get... complicated."

He stood up, brushing his hands on his dark pants.

"My name is Kaelen. And I am — or was — the guardian of this sword. Or I should have been. But it didn't choose me. It chose you."

"And what exactly am I supposed to do with a magical sword?" Aria crossed her arms. "Because I'm guessing there's an expectation involved here."

Kaelen studied her for a long moment, and Aria felt as though he were reading something written on her forehead that she couldn't see.

"There's a darkness approaching, Aria. Something ancient. Something that should have remained buried. The sword is the only thing that can stop it."

He paused.

"But first, you need to learn how to use it. And I'm the only person alive who can teach you."

Aria looked at the sword again. The blue glow seemed brighter now, as if responding to the conversation. She felt something — not a voice, not exactly, but a *presence*. A call.

It was absurd. It was impossible.

It was, somehow in a way she couldn't explain, the most certain thing she had ever felt.

"How long?" she asked at last.

Kaelen tilted his head.

"How long until the darkness arrives? Months. Maybe weeks, if the signs are correct."

"No," Aria rolled her eyes. "How long until I learn how to use this thing?"

A slow smile spread across Kaelen's face.

"A lifetime. But we can start with the basics this afternoon."

Aria took a deep breath, the forest air filling her lungs as it always had — familiar, safe, *hers*. But now there was something more in the wind, something that whispered of adventures and dangers and a destiny she had not asked for, but that somehow she had always known was waiting for her.

She approached the sword and, without hesitating this time, closed her fingers around the hilt.

The world exploded in blue light.
